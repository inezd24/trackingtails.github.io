Our Vision and Team

Who We Are

Project Nexus was founded on the principle that groundbreaking research should not remain confined to laboratories. We are a collective of scientists, engineers, and strategists dedicated to translating complex, theoretical concepts into tangible, sustainable solutions for the modern world.

Our mission is to foster a global network of innovators and drive technological adoption in sectors critical for future prosperity, including renewable energy, advanced materials, and ecological restoration.

Project Synopsis

Abstract

Domestic dogs, Canis familiaris, host diseases that cause a high burden to human and animal health in many parts of the world. Movement of dogs can be linked to disease transmission, and thus control may be achieved by movement regulation. However, the implementation and efficacy of such regulations is complicated by various environmental and socio-cultural factors. For domestic dogs that live close to humans, it is crucial for effective disease control to not only consider epidemiological aspects, but also to understand the dog husbandry and management systems and the dog-human relationship, while considering societal factors, such as economy, culture and religion in different communities. In this project, we will tackle the multifaceted human-animal-environment-disease interaction in free-roaming, individually or community owned dogs, and investigate the efficiency of socio-culturally adapted disease control strategies. We ask the following research questions: (i) What is the frequency and extent of local and regional dog movement and interaction, including human-mediated movement, and what are their drivers in different socio-cultural and geographical contexts? (ii) How are dog movement and contact patterns at the individual and population level linked to pathogen spread? (iii) How effective are movement restrictions in combination with other pathogen spread mitigation strategies, and what is their acceptability in different contexts? These questions can only be answered with an interdisciplinary approach that tightly integrates the scientific disciplines of veterinary epidemiology, social anthropology, population genetics and mathematical modelling. We will use existing and new data on dog contact networks (from GPS and contact sensor devices), pathogen occurrence (from serology and virome screening), practices of dog care and movement (from ethnographic methods, focus group discussions, key informant interviews, surveys), and dog population genetics to develop and calibrate mathematical and statistical models of dog movement and pathogen spread at individual and population levels. The integration of detailed data on behavioural variation of dog movement within populations and multi-generational movement patterns between populations, combined with information on pathogen diversity, will enable us to develop models that accurately capture multiple aspects of dog movement and behaviour as well as the epidemiology of canine diseases. Knowledge gained on the underlying system of dog movement will directly inform potential disease intervention strategies. We will explore the acceptability of these strategies with a participatory approach and simulate their effectiveness in reducing disease occurrence with mathematical models. The project consists of three work packages and 13 interrelated tasks, distributed amongst the four disciplines. We will conduct this research in four countries (Uganda, Chad, India, and Indonesia), which will enable us to identify cross-cultural and context-specific outcomes. Fieldwork in the four countries will be facilitated by extending collaborations with local academic partners and will be conducted in cross-discipline teams in a coordinated and collaborative way to optimize synergies. PhD students and postdocs, as well as academic personnel in the partner countries will be trained through the project. Scientific (through publications and conference contributions) and community outreach (through transdisciplinary stakeholder workshops) in each country will ensure impact on academia, national and international policy makers and on the society at large. With this project, we expect to make a large step forward in understanding the epidemiology of canine diseases, generate ground-breaking scientific outcomes, and at the same time make clear recommendations for socio-culturally adapted, sustainable intervention strategies that reduce disease burden in dogs and consequently in humans.
